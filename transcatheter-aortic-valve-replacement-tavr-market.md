<p><p>Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement (TAVR) is a minimally invasive procedure used to replace a narrowed aortic valve that fails to open properly, a condition known as aortic stenosis. This technique involves inserting a catheter through a small incision, typically in the femoral artery, allowing for the delivery of a new valve without the need for open-heart surgery. TAVR has gained popularity due to its reduced recovery time and lower risk of complications compared to traditional surgical approaches.</p><p>The TAVR market is experiencing significant growth driven by the increasing prevalence of aortic stenosis, an aging population, and a growing preference for less invasive treatment options. Technological advancements in valve designs and delivery systems have further enhanced the safety and efficacy of TAVR procedures. Additionally, favorable reimbursement policies and increasing awareness among patients and healthcare providers are contributing to market expansion. The market features several approaches: the transfemoral approach, which accesses the heart via the femoral artery, is the most common and suitable for many patients. The transapical approach involves access through the chest wall, often used for patients with unsuitable arteries. Other approaches include subclavian and direct aortic methods. Each approach caters to specific patient anatomies and clinical considerations, expanding treatment options in heart valve therapies.</p></p>
